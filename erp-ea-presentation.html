The motivation

ERP Projects Fail — Often, and Expensively

An ERP rollout is among the largest, riskiest IT investments a firm makes — multi-year, multi-million, touching every department.

Study after study reports that a large share of ERP projects overrun budget or schedule, and many never deliver the benefits that justified them.

Textbook cautionary tales: FoxMeyer Drug (drove the firm toward bankruptcy), Hershey's missed Halloween shipments (1999), Nike's supply-chain write-down. The software mostly worked — the organisational fit did not.

Quick recap

What an ERP System Is

One integrated software suite running on a single shared data model, serving many business functions at once.

Two defining promises:

Integration — one version of the truth across finance, HR, supply chain, sales.

Standardisation — the package ships with embedded "best-practice" processes.

Keep both promises in mind — each one is also a source of risk.

The core tension

An ERP Reshapes the Organisation

"The system imposes its own logic on a company's strategy, organisation, and culture."

— the classic warning, Davenport (1998), Harvard Business Review

You rarely bend the package fully to the firm. The firm bends — partly — to the package. Every implementation lives on one tension:

Standardise (adopt the package's process)  vs  Customise (rebuild it to fit you). Both carry cost and risk.

Diagnosis

Why They Fail — and What Kind of Problem It Really Is

Business–IT misalignment

The system does not serve the actual strategy or operating model.

Scope & customisation creep

Endless local tweaks; the package's coherence is lost.

Integration & data debt

Legacy systems and dirty master data break the "one truth" promise.

Unclear decision rights

No one knows who may decide standardise-vs-customise, or at what level.

Under-managed change

People and processes are not prepared for the new logic.

None of these is a coding bug. They are architecture problems — about how business, information, applications and technology fit together.

The lens

Enterprise Architecture

A holistic description of an enterprise's business and IT — plus the principles guiding their design and evolution — used to align IT with business strategy.

EA as a strategy for business–IT alignment: Lumor, Pulkkinen & Hirvonen (2019).

EA links strategy → business → information → applications → technology, and keeps an as-is and a to-be view to guide transformation.

Research-based · University of Jyväskylä

The EA Grid — Four Views × Three Levels

A practical map for EA decisions, developed and tested in large organisations implementing enterprise systems.

Hirvonen & Pulkkinen (2004); Pulkkinen (2006).

Four views: Business, Information, Application, Technology.

Three decision levels: Enterprise (strategy / top management), Domain (business operations), Systems (detailed IS design).

Each cell = a distinct decision, with its own owners and concerns — and one shared language across very different stakeholders.

The key insight

An ERP Lights Up the Whole Grid

A single ERP project is, at once:

a strategic bet (Enterprise · BA), a process redesign (Domain · BA), a master-data restructuring (IA), an application replacement & integration (AA), and a platform choice (TA).

That is exactly why ERP is so hard: one project crosses every cell — and most failures come from a decision made in one cell with no view of the others.

EA makes those crossings visible before they become problems.

Synthesis

Three Ways EA Supports ERP

1 · Alignment

Ties the ERP back to strategy and operating model, top-down through the Enterprise → Domain levels. Counters business–IT misalignment and scope drift.

2 · Coherence & Integration

As-is / to-be views across Information, Application and Technology expose data and integration dependencies before they bite (Pulkkinen 2006). Counters legacy & data debt.

3 · Governance

The three levels assign clear decision rights — who decides standardise-vs-customise, and where. EA practices keep the design reconfigurable as needs change (Lumor, Hirvonen & Pulkkinen 2021). Counters unclear authority & customisation creep.

What Went Wrong Here?

A manufacturer let each plant customise the ERP's procurement module to match its old local process.

Two years on, no two plants report numbers the same way, and a group-wide dashboard is impossible.

💬 Discuss with your neighbour

Which EA mechanism, applied earlier, would have prevented this — and at which grid level should the decision have sat?

Answer

Governance + Alignment

"Standardise vs customise procurement" is an Enterprise / Domain-level Business-Architecture decision.

It should never have been delegated down to each plant's Systems level. EA's decision-level structure assigns that right upward; alignment to a single group-reporting strategy would have constrained local customisation.

Reconfigurability (Lumor et al. 2021) still leaves room for genuine local needs — without losing one shared truth.
